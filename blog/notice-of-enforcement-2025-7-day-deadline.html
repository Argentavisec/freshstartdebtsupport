What Is a Notice of Enforcement?

A Notice of Enforcement is a formal letter sent by an enforcement agent (bailiff) to warn you that they have been instructed to collect a debt and may visit your home to take control of goods if you don‚Äôt deal with it. It‚Äôs not the very first letter about the debt ‚Äî but it is usually the first letter from the bailiff company itself.

It is usually the first letter you get from a bailiff company once a debt has reached ‚Äúcompliance stage‚Äù. Common companies that send NOEs include Marston Holdings, CDCBailiffs, Excel Civil Enforcement, Jacobs Enforcement, Bristow & Sutor, Newlyn, and others listed in our bailiffs menu.

Your NOE should usually show:

  • Who you owe (council, court, or other creditor)
  • The enforcement company‚Äôs name and reference number
  • The total amount owed, including the ¬£75 compliance fee
  • A deadline by which you must pay or contact them
  • What could happen if you do nothing (an enforcement visit and more fees)

Is a Notice of Enforcement the same as a CCJ?

No. A County Court Judgment (CCJ) is a court decision that you owe a debt. A Notice of Enforcement is a later step in the process, when a creditor or local authority has moved to enforcement and instructed bailiffs. You can have a Notice of Enforcement without realising there was a CCJ earlier, especially if you moved address or missed earlier letters.

NOE vs ‚Äúdebt collector‚Äù letters

Another common point of confusion is between debt collectors and bailiffs. Debt collectors write and call you but can‚Äôt take goods from your home; enforcement agents (bailiffs) can, once the legal steps are in place.

  • If the letter clearly states ‚ÄúNotice of Enforcement‚Äù and talks about taking control of goods, that‚Äôs a bailiff issue.

How the 7-Day Notice of Enforcement Deadline Works

In England and Wales, bailiffs must usually give you at least 7 clear days‚Äô notice between sending a Notice of Enforcement and visiting your home to take control of goods (except in some limited situations, such as certain criminal fines or urgent cases).

‚Äú7 clear days‚Äù in practice

‚ÄúClear days‚Äù normally means:

  • You do not count the day the notice is given
  • You do not count Sundays, Christmas Day, Good Friday or bank holidays
  • You do not count the day of the visit

This is meant to give you a genuine chance to get advice, check the debt and make an arrangement before any visit. Official guidance and debt advice charities confirm this minimum notice period for most civil debts.

How long after a Notice of Enforcement can bailiffs come?

People often search this exact question. In most cases, bailiffs can‚Äôt visit until the full 7 clear days have passed. After that, they can visit fairly quickly, sometimes within a day or two if they have capacity.

Which Debts Lead to a Notice of Enforcement?

Not every debt can lead straight to bailiffs. A Notice of Enforcement normally follows legal steps such as a liability order or court judgment. You are most likely to see an NOE for:

  • Council tax arrears (after a liability order) ‚Äî see Council Tax Debt Help
  • Parking and traffic penalties (PCNs) following warrants of control
  • Magistrates‚Äô court fines (including TV licence and some criminal fines)
  • Some business rates and other local authority debts
  • Certain High Court enforcement cases after a County Court Judgment or High Court writ

Can you get a Notice of Enforcement for credit cards and loans?

Ordinary credit cards, loans, overdrafts and catalogues don‚Äôt usually go to bailiffs straight away. Your lender normally has to:

  • Default the account and send warning letters
  • Potentially pass the account to a debt collection agency
  • Go to court and obtain a CCJ and then enforcement rights

Even then, many lenders prefer other options like attachment of earnings or charging orders rather than sending bailiffs. For those debts, see Stop Debt Collectors and our guides to firms like Lowell, Cabot and Moorcroft.

Notice of Enforcement for parking fines and PCNs

If your NOE relates to a parking ticket or traffic penalty, bailiffs will usually only become involved after the local authority has completed a specific process (including an Order for Recovery and warrant). If you‚Äôve moved house, it‚Äôs common for earlier letters to have gone to the old address, so your first awareness may be the Notice of Enforcement itself.

HMRC and tax Notices of Enforcement

For some HMRC tax debts, the process can look a bit different and can move quicker. HMRC has strong powers to enforce debts, including using enforcement officers, so it‚Äôs crucial not to ignore any NOE connected to tax.

What Happens If You Ignore a Notice of Enforcement?

If you ignore your NOE and take no action, the case will usually move from compliance stage to enforcement stage ‚Äî this is when things become more expensive and stressful.

  • A bailiff can visit your home to take control of goods.
  • An extra ¬£235 enforcement fee is added to your balance (on top of the ¬£75 compliance fee).
  • They may list items such as your car or household goods on a Controlled Goods Agreement.
  • If you break that agreement, they can return and move towards the sale/removal stage (with further fees).

Can a Notice of Enforcement be cancelled?

In some situations an NOE can be withdrawn, reissued or paused ‚Äî for example if:

  • The debt is plainly not yours (wrong person or address)
  • The amount is obviously wrong or has already been paid
  • The Notice of Enforcement was not served correctly
  • You‚Äôre clearly vulnerable and enforcement isn‚Äôt appropriate

However, most councils and enforcement firms will only cancel or suspend action if there‚Äôs a strong reason. In other cases, the best outcome is often to agree a fair plan and prevent things escalating to a visit and sale of goods.

Why ‚Äúdoing nothing‚Äù usually costs more

Ignoring the letter rarely makes it go away; it usually just makes it more expensive and stressful. The aim is to use the 7 days to either challenge anything that‚Äôs wrong or agree a plan you can truly afford, not a short-term promise that will fall over next month.

5 Steps to Take as Soon as You Get a Notice of Enforcement

1. Read the Letter Carefully

Check:

  • Who the creditor is (council, court, etc.)
  • The amount and whether it looks right
  • The deadline date and how to contact the enforcement company

If you‚Äôre unsure what the debt relates to, ask for a full breakdown. For council tax, you can also speak to the council‚Äôs revenues team directly and check the year(s) and address. If it might be someone else‚Äôs liability, challenge it quickly and in writing.

Once you‚Äôve done a quick budget, contact the enforcement firm and offer:

Can Bailiffs Visit Without Sending a Notice of Enforcement?

For most civil debts in England & Wales (such as council tax and parking penalties), bailiffs should send a Notice of Enforcement and allow the correct notice period before visiting. If you believe you have had a visit without any NOE, or the notice was not served correctly, you should not ignore it.

  • Ask the enforcement company when and how they say the NOE was sent
  • Request a copy of the notice and any records of delivery
  • Seek independent debt advice on challenging fees or action if rules were not followed

Different rules can apply for criminal fines, HMRC debts and urgent situations, which is why it helps to show your letter to a specialist and confirm your exact position. In some cases, incorrect service of the NOE can mean certain fees are not recoverable and may need to be refunded.

Notice of Enforcement ‚Äî Quick FAQ

Do I have to let bailiffs in after a Notice of Enforcement?

For most debts like council tax and PCNs, you do not have to let bailiffs in. They can usually only enter through a normal door that you open and invite them through. Once they have been inside and taken peaceful entry, different rules apply. See Can Bailiffs Force Entry in 2025?

Can I pay the council or court instead of the bailiffs?

Sometimes councils or courts will still accept payment, but once a debt is with enforcement agents they may insist that you deal with the bailiff company. Even then, you can still ask the creditor to step in if the enforcement company is being unreasonable or ignoring vulnerability.

What if I can‚Äôt afford what the bailiff wants?

Don‚Äôt agree to an amount that will leave you short on essentials. Instead, put forward what you can afford based on a realistic budget and seek advice if they refuse. In some cases, a formal debt solution can give you stronger protection and a single, manageable payment.

Can a Notice of Enforcement be cancelled?

In some cases a notice can be withdrawn or action paused (for example if the debt isn‚Äôt yours, has already been paid, or you‚Äôre clearly vulnerable). Other times, the best you can do is agree a fair plan and prevent things moving to a more serious stage. Either way, it‚Äôs worth asking for help early.

Will this affect my credit file?

The Notice of Enforcement itself doesn‚Äôt usually show on your credit file. However, the underlying debt (such as unpaid council tax or a court judgment) may already be recorded, and some solutions used to deal with the situation (like IVAs) will affect your credit rating. We‚Äôll explain all the trade-offs before you decide.

Is a Notice of Enforcement a criminal matter?

Most council tax and PCN Notices of Enforcement are civil, not criminal. However, some magistrates‚Äô fines and HMRC issues can have criminal elements. That doesn‚Äôt mean you‚Äôre a criminal for falling behind, but it does mean you should take the letters very seriously and seek help quickly.

Payment Arrangement After a Summons — How to Get a Plan Agreed

If you’ve had a summons or even a liability order, you can often still set up an affordable payment arrangement. Councils generally want realistic, defendable offers backed by a budget and—crucially—ongoing payment of the current year’s instalments so the arrears don’t grow. See impartial explainer on timelines and options via Citizens Advice and legal steps on GOV.UK.

What to send (written contact wins)

  • Your council tax account number and any liability order reference(s).
  • A short note asking for written contact only while you complete your budget.
  • A defendable offer: current year + monthly arrears amount you can keep up.
Tip: Keep copies of everything. Many councils provide online “make an offer” forms post-summons/liability order—use them and upload your budget.

Liability Order — What It Means & What Happens Next

A liability order gives the council stronger recovery powers if you don’t pay. After it’s granted, many councils will still agree a plan if you engage quickly in writing with a realistic budget and offer. Typical next steps include enforcement agents, Attachment of Earnings, or deductions from benefits; councils outline this process (e.g., Leeds City Council).

Possible actions after a liability order

  • Asking enforcement agents (bailiffs) to collect
  • Deductions from wages/benefits (Attachment of Earnings)
  • For larger debts, insolvency or charging order (less common)

You’re legally obliged to return any “financial information” form many councils send after a liability order—usually within 14 days.

Council Tax Bailiff Fees 2025 — The Real Numbers (and How to Avoid Them)

Under the Taking Control of Goods (Fees) Regulations 2014, the common fee stages are £75 (compliance/Notice of Enforcement), £235 + 7.5% over £1,500 (enforcement visit), and £110 + 7.5% over £1,500 (sale). Government has consulted on a 5% uplift; until implemented, the 2014 levels underpin most guidance. The cheapest route is to agree an arrangement during the compliance letter stage to avoid the visit fee. See rights guidance on GOV.UK and practical “keep them outside” steps via Citizens Advice.

Attachment of Earnings (AEO) — How to Stop or Reduce Wage Deductions

If a court-ordered Attachment of Earnings is taking money from your wages for council tax arrears and it’s unaffordable, you can usually apply to vary or suspend it by showing a realistic income & expenditure and evidence (see AEO guidance). Local authority employer guides (e.g., Bolton Council) explain how deductions are calculated. If you set up a formal plan (IVA/DRO), deductions may change—ask the court or council on your order.

Breathing Space for Council Tax Arrears — 60-Day Legal Pause

Breathing Space (Debt Respite Scheme) can pause most enforcement and interest/charges for up to 60 days while you get debt advice and set up a plan. Council tax arrears are qualifying debts. It’s started by a debt adviser and doesn’t remove your liability, but it buys time to get an arrangement or solution in place. See official info on GOV.UK and practical explainers via StepChange and National Debtline.

Section 13A Discretionary Hardship Relief — One-Off Help from Your Council

Alongside standard discounts, councils can grant a Section 13A discretionary reduction where you’re in exceptional hardship. It’s separate from Council Tax Reduction (CTR) and can reduce bills or help with arrears. National policy notes and directions were updated in 2024; check your council’s page and provide evidence of income, essential costs and why other options aren’t enough. (Background: DLUHC direction on s13A(1)(c) use, 2024.)

Check 13A Help

Council Tax Reduction (CTR) & 25% Single Person Discount — Don’t Miss These

CTR (means-tested) can reduce your bill—sometimes up to 100% depending on local schemes. Apply through your local council. If you’re the only adult (18+) in your home, check the 25% Single Person Discount; you can receive both if eligible. See GOV.UK — who pays & discounts and typical council pages (e.g., Central Bedfordshire).

Vulnerability & Reasonable Adjustments — Get Communication That Works for You

If health, disability, caring responsibilities or other circumstances make it hard to deal with enforcement, tell the council/bailiff company. Request reasonable adjustments (e.g., written contact, slower pace, third-party liaison). National guidance and reforms emphasise protecting the financially vulnerable; see rights on bailiff conduct at GOV.UK. Campaign data also shows rising bailiff use, strengthening the case for fair collection in hardship.

Council Tax Debt — Extra FAQs (Quick Answers)

Can I spread council tax over 12 months?

Yes—ask your council to switch to 12 monthly instalments to lower each payment. This can run alongside an arrears arrangement.

Can I go to prison for council tax?

Imprisonment is rare and only after a separate means enquiry if the court decides you “wilfully refused or culpably neglected” to pay. Engage early and keep written proof of offers.

Can council tax arrears be written off?

Sometimes. Councils have discretionary powers (e.g., Section 13A) and formal debt solutions (IVA/DRO) can write off eligible unsecured debts on completion—get tailored advice first.

Will bailiffs force entry for council tax?

Not on a first visit for council tax. Keep doors locked, talk through the door, and move to a written plan.

What if my income is variable?

Use an average with headroom; DMPs flex more, IVAs have variation processes. We’ll help set a payment you can keep.

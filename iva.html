What Is an IVA?

If you’ve been searching for “What is an IVA?” or even the trending term “What is a IVA UK”, you’re not alone. Thousands of people across the country are looking for safe, legal ways to deal with unaffordable debt — and an IVA could be the answer.

An Individual Voluntary Arrangement (IVA) is a government-approved debt solution that lets you reduce your monthly payments and write off remaining debt after a fixed term. It’s legally binding, protects you from bailiffs, and is managed by a licensed insolvency practitioner.

Once approved, your creditors must stop contacting you. Interest is frozen, legal action is halted, and you pay only what you can afford each month — usually for 5 or 6 years.

Who Can Get an IVA?

Not everyone is eligible, but an IVA is ideal if you meet these criteria:

  • You owe over £6,000 in unsecured debt
  • You have debts with at least two different creditors
  • You live in England, Wales or Northern Ireland
  • You have a regular income — from employment, self-employment, or benefits

Debts You Can Include in an IVA

Most unsecured debts are eligible to be included in an IVA. These typically include:

  • Credit card debts
  • Personal loans
  • Council tax arrears
  • Gas, electric, and water bills
  • Bank overdrafts
  • Payday loans
  • Catalogue and store card debt
  • Self-employed tax or HMRC debts

If you're being chased by bailiffs or enforcement agents, an IVA can stop legal action almost immediately.

How Much Debt Can I Write Off?

In most cases, an IVA allows you to write off a significant portion of your debt. Based on our client data from 2024–2025, the average debt write-off is between 60% and 85%.

Example:

  • Total Debt: £24,000
  • Monthly Payment Before IVA: £600+
  • New IVA Monthly Payment: £120
  • Debt Written Off: ~£18,000

Every case is different, but the savings can be life-changing.

IVA vs Other Debt Options

The UK offers a range of debt solutions. An IVA is just one — here’s how it compares:

  • DMP (Debt Management Plan) – Informal and flexible, but creditors can still chase you.
  • DRO (Debt Relief Order) – Good for low income, low asset individuals.
  • Bankruptcy – Drastic but quick. Can affect your home or career.

Individual Voluntary Arrangement (IVA)

An Individual Voluntary Arrangement (IVA) is one of the most popular formal debt solutions in the UK, allowing you to legally write off a portion of your debt while making affordable monthly repayments over a set period (usually 5 or 6 years). It’s approved by the courts and managed by a licensed insolvency practitioner.

With an IVA, you can often write off up to 85% of your unsecured debt if your circumstances qualify. Once it’s approved, all interest and charges are frozen, and creditors can’t take further legal action or send bailiffs. Your payments are based entirely on what you can afford.

Key Benefits of an IVA:

  • ✅ Write off unaffordable debt legally
  • ✅ Stop creditor harassment and bailiff action
  • ✅ Freeze interest and charges
  • ✅ Make one affordable monthly payment
  • ✅ Protect your home (if you own one)
  • ✅ Fixed timeframe – usually 5 years

Who is eligible for an IVA?

You may qualify for an IVA if:

  • You live in England, Wales or Northern Ireland
  • You owe over £6,000 in unsecured debts (e.g. credit cards, loans, overdrafts, Klarna, etc.)
  • You owe money to two or more creditors
  • You have a regular income (employed or self-employed)

Common debts included in an IVA:

  • Credit card debt
  • Catalogue debt
  • Loan debt (including personal loans and payday loans)
  • Klarna and Buy Now Pay Later debt
  • Utility arrears
  • Overdrafts
  • Store cards and more

While IVAs do appear on your credit file and public insolvency register, many people find it a small price to pay for the relief of becoming debt-free legally.

Debt Management Plan (DMP)

A Debt Management Plan (DMP) is an informal debt solution that allows you to repay your unsecured debts through one monthly payment based on what you can afford. It’s a flexible way to manage your finances without committing to insolvency or legal arrangements.

With a DMP, your payment is divided between your creditors, and many lenders agree to freeze interest and charges to help you get back on track. It’s commonly used for credit card debt, catalogue accounts, payday loans, and overdrafts.

Key Benefits of a DMP:

  • ✅ One monthly affordable payment
  • ✅ Interest and charges often frozen
  • ✅ Stops harassing calls and letters
  • ✅ No court involvement required
  • ✅ Can be adjusted if your income changes

Who is a DMP suitable for?

You might benefit from a DMP if:

  • You’re struggling to meet minimum payments
  • You have multiple debts like credit cards, loans, and catalogues
  • You want to avoid formal insolvency options
  • You want to protect your credit rating as much as possible

Debts commonly included in a DMP:

  • Credit card balances
  • Catalogue debts (e.g. Very, Littlewoods)
  • Buy Now Pay Later (Klarna, Clearpay)
  • Personal loans
  • Overdrafts
  • Payday loans

DMP Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Flexible and non-legally binding
  • No court records or insolvency register
  • Can be changed or cancelled at any time

Cons:

  • No legal protection from court action (unlike an IVA or DRO)
  • May take longer to repay than other solutions
  • Creditors can refuse or withdraw from the arrangement

Many people start with a DMP as a way to regain control quickly, then later consider moving to a more permanent solution like an IVA once their situation stabilises.

Debt Relief Order (DRO)

A Debt Relief Order (DRO) is a low-cost debt solution designed for individuals with low income, minimal assets, and relatively low debt levels. It allows you to write off your debts completely after 12 months — if your financial situation hasn’t improved in that time. During the 12-month “moratorium” period, you make no payments at all.

DROs are ideal for people who simply cannot afford to repay their debts and don’t own a house or significant assets. It offers legal protection from creditors and stops all enforcement action, interest, and charges.

Key Benefits of a DRO:

  • ✅ Full debt write-off after 12 months
  • ✅ No monthly payments required
  • ✅ Stops bailiffs and court action
  • ✅ Interest and charges are frozen
  • ✅ Costs only £90 total to apply

Who qualifies for a DRO?

You may be eligible for a DRO if you:

  • Owe less than £30,000 in total unsecured debt (as of 2021 regulations)
  • Have under £75 monthly disposable income
  • Don’t own your home
  • Have less than £2,000 in total assets
  • Live in England, Wales or Northern Ireland

Debts included in a DRO:

  • Credit cards
  • Catalogue accounts
  • Personal loans and payday loans
  • Utility arrears
  • Overdrafts
  • Council tax arrears

DRO Pros and Cons

Pros:

  • Ideal for low income or benefits claimants
  • Legal debt write-off in just 12 months
  • Protection from creditor action and court enforcement

Cons:

  • Strict eligibility rules
  • Appears on the insolvency register for 15 months
  • Credit rating will be negatively impacted

Many people on Universal Credit or with no surplus income find that a DRO is the fastest and cheapest route to becoming debt-free legally.

Bankruptcy

Bankruptcy is a formal insolvency solution designed for individuals who are unable to repay their debts. It offers a clean slate by legally writing off most unsecured debts, giving you a chance to start over financially. While it has serious consequences, it may be the right option for some people who have exhausted all alternatives.

Bankruptcy typically lasts 12 months. During this time, your finances are handled by an Official Receiver. Once the process is complete, most of your debts are legally written off — including credit cards, loans, overdrafts, council tax, and more.

Key Benefits of Bankruptcy:

  • ✅ Legally write off most unsecured debts
  • ✅ Stops bailiffs and all legal action
  • ✅ Interest and charges are frozen
  • ✅ Usually lasts only 1 year
  • ✅ Fresh financial start with no ongoing repayments (in most cases)

How to declare bankruptcy in the UK

You can apply for bankruptcy online through the Insolvency Service. It costs £680, which must be paid before your application is reviewed. If approved, you will be declared bankrupt and all enforcement action against you will stop.

Debts that can be written off with bankruptcy:

  • Credit cards and loans
  • Klarna and BNPL debts
  • Payday loans
  • Overdrafts
  • Council tax arrears
  • Utility bills
  • Catalogue and store card debts

What are the downsides of bankruptcy?

  • You may have to sell assets like your home or car (if valuable)
  • Your credit score will be affected for 6 years
  • You’ll appear on the public insolvency register
  • You may have restrictions placed on your finances during the bankruptcy
  • Bankruptcy may affect employment in certain professions (e.g., legal or financial roles)

Bankruptcy vs IVA

While both solutions write off debt, an IVA allows you to keep your assets and make manageable monthly payments — whereas bankruptcy may involve asset loss but no ongoing repayments (unless you have surplus income). Many choose an IVA over bankruptcy to protect their home or career.

Bankruptcy can feel like a last resort, but for some, it’s a powerful tool to reset their finances and eliminate unmanageable debt for good.

Trust Deed (Scotland Only)

A Trust Deed is a formal debt solution available only in Scotland. It’s very similar to an IVA, offering legal protection from creditors and the chance to write off unaffordable debt. A Protected Trust Deed (PTD) is legally binding and must be set up by a licensed insolvency practitioner.

Once protected, creditors can no longer contact you, add interest, or take enforcement action. You’ll make one affordable monthly payment for 4 years, after which any remaining debt is written off.

Key Benefits of a Protected Trust Deed:

  • ✅ Write off debt legally after 4 years
  • ✅ Stop interest, charges, and legal action
  • ✅ Make one affordable monthly payment
  • ✅ Protects your home and assets (in most cases)
  • ✅ No more pressure from creditors or debt collectors

Who qualifies for a Trust Deed in Scotland?

You may be eligible for a Trust Deed if:

  • You live in Scotland
  • You owe over £5,000 in unsecured debts
  • You have multiple creditors
  • You can afford a reasonable monthly contribution

Common debts included:

  • Credit cards
  • Loans (including payday loans)
  • Klarna & BNPL debt
  • Overdrafts
  • Council tax and utility bills
  • Store cards and catalogue accounts

Trust Deed vs IVA:

An IVA is available in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. A Trust Deed is for people in Scotland only. Both offer legal protection and debt write-off — but a Trust Deed usually lasts 4 years instead of 5 or 6 with an IVA.

If you live in Scotland and are struggling with debt, a Protected Trust Deed could be your most effective way to regain control.
